The Resilience of Coastal Mangroves

Mangroves, the tangled forests that straddle the boundary between land and sea, have long been dismissed as malarial wastelands. Yet a growing body of research is rehabilitating their reputation. These salt-tolerant trees, it turns out, shed light on one of the most pressing questions of our age: how can vulnerable coastlines weather the onslaught of rising seas and intensifying storms?

In the wake of the 2004 Indian Ocean tsunami, villages shielded by dense mangrove belts suffered markedly less damage than those whose mangroves had been cleared for shrimp farms. The trees' labyrinthine roots dissipate wave energy, trap sediment, and curb erosion. They also sequester carbon at rates that put many terrestrial forests to shame — by some estimates, four times more per hectare than rainforests.

Despite these virtues, mangroves continue to be felled at an alarming clip. Conservationists, undaunted, are pioneering schemes that pay coastal communities to restore the trees, reframing them not as obstacles to development but as bulwarks against catastrophe. Whether such efforts can outpace the bulldozers remains an open question — but the calculus, increasingly, favors the mangroves.
